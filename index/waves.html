First Wave (1800s-early 1900s) Focused on legal rights, especially women's right to vote and own property. Leaders like Susan B. Anthony and Emmeline Pankhurst fought for women's suffrage, paving the way for future change.

Second Wave (1960s-1980s) Centered on social and workplace equality. Feminists demanded equal pay, reproductive rights, and freedom from gender-based violence. Slogans like “The personal is political” reminded people that everyday issues — family, work, body autonomy — were also political

Third Wave (1990s-2000s) Challenged the idea that there was only one kind of feminism. It celebrated diversity, individuality, and intersectionality — recognizing that gender issues are connected to race, sexuality, and culture.

Fourth Wave (2010-today) The current wave uses digital activism and social media to spread awareness and fight sexism, harassment, and inequality globally. Movements like #MeToo and #TimesUp are examples of modern feminism in action. Each wave built on the last, showing that feminism continues to grow and adapt to the worl's changing needs.
